Lattice Gas (Fluid Flow)

A Lattice Gas Automaton, or LGA, is used to model fluid flow and diffusion. Particles travel along lattice lines and collide at nodes. The collisions follow rules that obey mass and momentum conservation. This particular LGA is frequently called an "FHP lattice gas" after Frisch, Hasslacher, and Pomeau who showed that it reproduces the Navier-Stokes equations that describe fluid flow.

How's it work? Each cell holds a collection of "water" particles in a vector (or array) of length 6. Each element of the vector is a 0 or a 1. A 1 indicates the presence of a particle. Each index of the vector indicates the neighbor towards which the particle is moving. For example, if a cell has value [1, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1], then it has three particles which are moving towards neighbors 1, 3, and 6. The CA rule consists of two steps: (1) translation to neighboring nodes, and (2) collision. Translation just transfers the particles to their neighboring nodes; and subsequently, if more than one particle arrives at the same node, then they collide. Collisions are just a rearrangement of the particles so that the 1's change their indices in the vector. For example, if three particles arrived at a node as [1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0], then a collision might move them around to become [0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1].  Most importantly, the collisions are designed so that there is no change in mass and no change in momentum (inviolate conservation laws from physics). For more details on LGA and their rules, see my class notes on LGA or see one of the many other online references.

Note that the graphics do not show individual particle vectors, but instead show the vector sum of all particles that share a node. For example, if two particles originate from the same node but are traveling in opposite directions, then their vector sum is zero, and nothing will be displayed. Vectors that cancel each other's contributions occur frequently. However, sparsely populated grids will (in general) show the movement of distinct particles because there are fewer particles sharing nodes.

Fluid flow is best seen by keeping a running average of the particle's vectors over time (set on the Properties panel).
