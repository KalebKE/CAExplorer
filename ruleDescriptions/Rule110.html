Rule 110.

A very simple one-dimensional 2-state rule that has been proven capable of universal computation by Matthew Cook. In other words, this ridiculously simple rule is capable of calculating anything that can be computed by the world's most powerful supercomputers. Intuitively, you can see information being transmitted by "spaceships" (or gliders) of various speeds which collide and produce other spaceships in a manner analogous to electrons moving through logic gates. The logic gates could be used to build a computer. However, the actual proof converts the CA to a "cyclic tag system" which is known to be equivalent to a Universal Turing Machine.

What are some of the spaceships that "transmit" information? Look for patterns of triangles that meander through the background pattern. The ever-present background pattern is a repeating ...00010011011111... This pattern cycles back to the initial value every 7 time steps. Spaceships move through this background. For example,

  • Spaceship 1: 111 surrounded by the above background. Moves straight downwards and repeats every 6 time steps.
  • Spaceship 2: 1001111 surrounded by the above background. Moves left and repeats every 30 time steps.
  • Spaceship 3: 0001110111 surrounded by the above background. Moves right and repeats every 3 time steps.
  • Spaceship n: There are many others.

The "110" comes from a cellular automata numbering scheme popularized by Stephen Wolfram.  For details, see Wolfram 1-d Rules.  In essence, Rule 110 has the following mapping

  • 111 -> 0
  • 110 -> 1
  • 101 -> 1
  • 100 -> 0
  • 011 -> 1
  • 010 -> 1
  • 001 -> 1
  • 000 -> 0

In other words, if the cell and its neighbors are 111, then at the next time step the cell will become a 0.  If the cell and its neighbors are 110, then at the next time step the cell will become a 1.  Etc.  All total, this corresponds to the binary number 01101110 which is the number 110 in decimal.

Curious about other rule numbers? You can specify any Wolfram rule number by selecting and running the "Wolfram 1-d Rule" in the Rule tab.
