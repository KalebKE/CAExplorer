Minority Wins

A rebellious model of decision making where a person always chooses to go with the minority opinion. For example, a cell (or person) will decide to join a particular political party only if that party is in the minority among their neighbors (excluding their own opinion). If two neighbors are colored blue and six neighbors are colored red, then the cell will decide to join the minority blue party. In other words the cell will turn blue. If there is a tie, the party is picked randomly.

This simulation will flip-flop annoyingly as if the cells can never make up their mind. While hurtful to the eye, this is  the way birds behave when standing on the ground in a big flock.  Each birds is always changing its mind about whether or not they should eat or watch for predators. If all their neighboring birds in the flock are watching for predators (like hawks), then they will eat. And if all the neighboring birds are eating, then they will decide to watch out for predators because nobody else is watching out for them.  For more information try reading "Bahr, D. B. and M. Bekoff. 1999. Predicting Flock Vigilance From Simple Passerine Interactions: Modelling with Cellular Automata. Animal Behaviour, 58, 831-839."

This rule is very similar to Majority Wins, but the cell chooses the minority rather than the majority. Understanding this CA will probably be easier after studying the Majority Wins CA. As with Majority wins, this CA is very sensitive to the initial numbers in each of the parties. Try random populations with 45%, 50%, and 55% red cells.

To avoid a headache when using two-dimensional grids, try averaging 2 time steps. The average boundary behavior is fascinating near the phase change (50% red cells). Also try averaging 2, 3, or 4 time steps with 3 states and a 66% initial population. The physicists and mathematical sociologists out there will think this is way cool! There is some deep behavior being delineated by this averaging.
