The Game of Life

A mesmerizing simulation invented by John Conway and popularized in Martin Gardner's Scientific American article, Mathematical Games (1970). No players are involved in this "game". Just set up an initial state and watch Life evolve.

Cells are either occupied (live) or unoccupied (dead) and evolve according to the following rules.

  • A live cell with fewer than 2 live neighbors dies of loneliness.
  • A live cell with 4 or more live neighbors dies of overcrowding.
  • A live cell with exactly 2 or 3 live neighbors survives unchanged.
  • A dead cell with exactly 3 live neighbors will come to life (birth).
On a square grid with 8 neighbors, "Life" is capable of universal computation. In other words, with the correct initial conditions, "Life" can calculate anything that your desktop computer can calculate, albeit more slowly. Wow!
