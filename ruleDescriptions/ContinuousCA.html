Continuous CA

Amazingly "discrete" behavior emerging from cells with real ("continuous") values.  This CA appears capable of mimicking many integer-valued CA, including many of the one-dimensional Wolfram Rules with their characteristic upside down triangles.

Each cell is averaged with all of its neighbors.  The average x is converted to y = m x + b for some slope m and intercept b.  Then, only the fractional part of y is kept (the whole number part is discarded, in a sort of real-valued version of "modulo 1.0").  This is the new value assigned to the cell.

Be sure to open the More Properties box and adjust the slope and intercept.  In particular, to get a sense of the possible behaviors, try m = 1.0 and b = 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, ..., 0.9, 1.0 with a one-dim (2 neighbor) lattice.  Notice that these encompass all of the Wolfram classes I, II, III, and IV.  Also try the other suggestions in the More Properties box.

Any integer-valued rule can be approximated by a real-valued rule, but Stephen Wolfram in A New Kind of Science has further hypothesized that real-valued (or complex, or etc.) rules do not confer any additional non-trivial behaviors beyond those already created by integer rules.  In other words, Wolfram believes that classes I, II, III, and IV still apply to rules built from real numbers.  At the very least, it is interesting to see integer-rule behaviors reproduced so nicely in real-valued rules.  Or perhaps it's vice-versa.
